Lion

Scientific Name: Panthera leo
Size: Head and body, 4.5 to 6.5 feet; tail, 26.25 to 39.5 inches
Weight: 265 to 420 pounds
Speed: 81 km/h (50.3 mph)
Lions are one of the only big cats species that live together in group settings, called a pride. These large cats are infamously known for work as cooperative team to plan their strategic tactics, to obtain a meal. Male cubs stay with the pride up until the age of 2-3 years old. The juvenile males are either chased out by the domain male lions or chased out by a new pride male, that has viciously mauled the former male lion, during a pride takeover. Just, because he has won the war, doesn't mean he has won the battle with the lionesses. If it's coalition of brothers, it's very dangerous for lionesses during pride takeover. Regardless of this they will defend their cubs with there lives, even going as far as tricking the male lion into being interested in mating, to give there pride a chance to get away from dangerous males, which will kill cubs to through, the existing adult lioness into heat. Male lions that take over a pride will also chase off juvenile lioness. A pride is a tight unit family, with mothers, daughters, sisters, cousins, and young. As this is the core foundation of a pride, male lions come and go in there lives. With pride, hunts are more successful, than a lone lion, and defending one another from hyenas always tips the scale with there dangerous fight to hold onto a meal.
Fun Fact: Lion's roar can be heard from five miles away.

Leopard

Scientific Name: Panthera pardus
Size: Head and body: 4.25 to 6.25 feet; tail: 3.5 to 4.5 feet
Weight: 66 to 176 pounds
Speed: 56-60 km/h (35-37 mph)
Leopards are known for their elusive reclusive behavior of not being heard, but seen till it's too late. These are solitary hunters that dwell in thick brushes of the savannahs. However, they have some of the best tactics when it comes to hunting, such as, there way of using gravity to land itself a meal. With there rotating ankles/ wrists, they can climb up and down a tree flawlessly. This comes into a key role of nimbleness within the branches catching prey from there as well. They are also innovative, when comes to droughts(Africa's dry season), as they will also go fishing for catfish, in the drying up ponds/rivers. There are pros to being solitary hunters, however, being solitary have its cons, they are more likely to lose their prey to larger animal, if they haven't secured there prey up a tree, moments after a hunt, it would be taken usually by the optimistic hyena. With that note, cubs soully depend upon there mother for everything, such as, protection and meals. Which leaves them vulnerable when there mother is away hunting, which in return yields more cub deaths, as each predator seeks out each others cubs, to reduce the competition for prey resources. It's a 2/5 chance a leopard cub would make it to adolescents, and the adolescents making it to adulthood.
Fun Fact: The dark spots on their bodies are called rosettes.
